## POST 1: Your AI agent needs a primary luminary

We give our AI agents luminaries — the specific thinkers whose work informs their discipline. The Architect boots knowing Simon Brown and Michael Nygard. The Designer boots knowing Don Norman and Sophia Prater.

But we discovered that a flat list of influences produces flat output. When Norman says "make the right action obvious" and Prater says "derive screens from domain objects," the agent compromises. It produces something that's sort of obvious and sort of entity-derived. Neither fish nor fowl.

The fix: a **primary luminary**. One thinker whose perspective dominates the iteration as a tiebreaker.

Set "Don Norman" as the primary luminary, and affordance wins every close call. Set "Ellen Lupton," and storytelling hierarchy takes precedence. Set "Sophia Prater," and navigation mirrors the domain model.

The other luminaries don't disappear. They still inform. The primary is the tiebreaker when approaches conflict — and in UX, approaches always conflict.

Here's what this unlocks: **prototype sprints**. Before committing to a full build, the agent produces 2-3 lightweight visual variants, each driven by a different primary luminary. You view them locally. You pick a direction. Then Construction builds the one you chose.

This is how professional design teams work. They don't converge on the first idea. They diverge first — multiple directions, explore the space — then converge on the strongest.

The AI agent equivalent: give it 3 different thinkers, get 3 different prototypes. The taste isn't in the agent. It's in the selection.

## POST 2: The prototype sprint — see it before you build it

Yesterday our AI system went from screenshots to working CRM in one session. Inception → Elaboration → Construction → merged code. Impressive. And also a problem.

The operator only saw the result *after* it was fully built. Five feature branches, ~103 tests, a complete Outreach page. If the aesthetic direction was wrong, all of that work is wasted.

So we built a prototype sprint.

Before the full Elaboration phase — before the Architect writes ADRs, before the Designer runs the UX translation chain — the agent produces 2-3 lightweight prototypes. Static HTML. Tailwind via CDN. Stubbed data. No framework, no database, no build step. Viewable by opening a file in your browser.

Each prototype is driven by a different primary luminary:

**Variant A (Don Norman)**: Affordance-first. Every button self-explains. Minimal chrome. The right action is obvious; the wrong action is impossible.

**Variant B (Ellen Lupton)**: Storytelling hierarchy. The page reads like a narrative. Visual weight guides your eye through a sequence. Typography does the heavy lifting.

**Variant C (Sophia Prater)**: Entity-derived. Navigation mirrors the domain model. Screens derive from objects, not features. The information architecture IS the interface.

The operator picks a direction — or combines elements. "Variant B, but with Norman's button clarity." That selection becomes the iteration's primary luminary. The Designer's Elaboration work, the Builder's Construction code, and the Reviewer's quality checks all operate through that lens.

Time to produce three variants: ~10 turns. Time saved by not building the wrong thing: immeasurable.

## POST 3: Your design system has a color psychology gap

We spent two weeks building a design governance system for AI agents. Creative Director with luminaries. Design token governance. Usability checks. Contrast ratios. Typography hierarchy.

Then we realized: we had color *theory* but no color *psychology*.

Josef Albers taught our Creative Director that color is relative — the same blue reads differently next to gray than next to orange. That's perception. It answers: "How does this color look here?"

But nobody was answering: "How does this color make the user feel?"

That's a different question. And it's answered by different thinkers:

**Eva Heller** — empirical research on color-emotion associations across cultures. Her work shows that blue universally signals trust, but the specific shade matters enormously. Hospital blue ≠ banking blue ≠ social media blue.

**Faber Birren** — age-based color preferences. Children prefer warm, saturated colors. Adults shift toward cooler, desaturated palettes. If your app serves both (like ours — a family investment tool used by adults and eventually their kids), your color system needs to accommodate both.

**Johannes Itten** — seven types of color contrast. Not just light/dark and warm/cool, but also complementary, simultaneous, saturation, and extension contrasts. Each produces a different psychological effect.

We added all three to our Creative Director's luminaries. Now when the agent chooses badge colors, status indicators, or background tones, it's not just checking contrast ratios — it's considering emotional resonance.

The gap between "accessible" and "effective" is color psychology. Most design systems stop at accessible.
